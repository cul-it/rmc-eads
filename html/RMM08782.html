Content Description

The collection consists of manuscripts, correspondence, printed ephemera, artwork, three-dimensional artifacts and photographs related to the joint career and lives of Irene and Vernon Castle (1911–1917) and to Irene's life and career after Vernon's death in February of 1918, including some of her animal welfare activism. Approximately 1,500 photographs and 1,000 manuscript pages comprise the bulk of the collection. Personal photographs include Irene during her childhood, family and home life, Irene with her pets, and travels around the world. Professional photographs were created by a variety of photographers and studios including Ira Hill, Frank Geisler, Campbell Studio, Charlotte Fairchild, Ray Huff, the Crosbys, Baron de Meyer, and Underwood Studios. These images include fashion poses, dance poses, and photos taken on film sets. Photographs also include dance poses of Vernon Castle, images of him during WWI as a pilot and his funeral procession, images of Irene with dance partner Billy Reardon and husbands Robert E. Treman, Frederic McLaughlin and George Enzinger. Manuscript material includes clippings from magazines and newspapers, music score sheets, artwork, letters and telegrams from Vernon to Irene and other family correspondence and condolence letters on the occasion of Vernon's death. The collection also includes 3D material relating to the Castles' career, including a silver trophy given to them by Jim Europe's orchestra (James Reese Europe) and a piece of aluminum rudder from Vernon's plane and medals he acquired during WWI.
Biographical / Historical

Dancer, actor, and fashion icon and designer Irene Castle (1893–1969) was born in New Rochelle, New York. One of the great stars of the silent-film era, she was adept at using photography to cultivate her image and cinema to promote her endeavors in the fashion world. Castle first became famous with her dance partner and husband Vernon Castle. With Vernon and after his death in WWI, she acted in a number of films produced by Ithaca's Wharton Studio and others. In 1919 she married the prominent Ithaca businessman Robert E. Treman, and the pair lived in what is now the Sigma Chi fraternity house until their separation in 1923.

Series II. Professional Photographs
Subseries IIa. Bound Volumes by Castle
Scope and Contents
These albums were created by Castle circa 1920 after her second marriage to Robert Treman of Ithaca, NY. Volumes 1-3 are stamped on the cover "IRENE CASTLE TREMAN." Most of the photographs in these albums were taken between 1913-1918, when she and Vernon were at the peak of their fame (although no photos of Vernon are included). Single photos are pasted on one side only. Occasionally, Castle added explanatory text or clippings from magazines to the pages, which provide some context for the images. When planning her autobiography, "Castles in the Air", Castle apparently removed some pages of images from the albums in order to submit them to the publisher for consideration. She added paper tags with typed explanatory captions to the bottom of many of those pages, though many detached over time. Loose pages from these albums are housed together.

Box 8
Professional photograph album documenting silent filmmaking, mainly "The Firing Line"
 1917-1922
Scope and Contents
The album begins with photographs of two dogs, as well as several photographs of Robert Treman and their Ithaca house. Most of the photographs in this album document the making of the 1919 feature, The Firing Line, the first and most successful film Castle made for Famous Players-Lasky/Paramount. The Firing Line was primarily shot in Miami Beach with some of the scenes shot in Fort Lee, New Jersey; the final scenes were filmed in the Adirondacks while she was on her honeymoon with Robert E. Treman. The album begins with photographs of dogs and Vernon's german shepherd, as well as several photographs of Robert Treman and their Ithaca house. There are shots of the directors, cinematographers, crew, and other actors, including leading men, both on the set and off. There are scenes of wildlife and several photographs of Seminole Indian men wearing characteristic "patchwork" garments.

150 small black and white photographs, captioned by Vernon. Included in this album are: images of Manhasset, NY, one depicting Irene on horse "Minto" while filming "The Whirl of Life."; Vernon's life as Royal Flying Corps pilot during WWI, some in France, most in Camp Mohawk, Canada and Camp Benbrook, Texas; planes that Vernon flew, as well as friends such as Johnny Coats, Eardley Wilmot and Gewn Wilmot; images of plane crashes; visits from Irene Castle in England and Canada, and in Englewood, New Jersey; family members, including Coralie and husband Lawrence Grossmith, Elroy and her children; Vernon's German Shepherd "Tell" and his monkey "Jeffrey"; Gladqyn MacDougal; Vernon's cars, including Minerva decorated by Elsie de Wolfe, a Stutz Bearcat, and the Marmon. The last pages of this album contain photographs of the plane crash that killed Vernon. These photographs were sent by Victor Dodd, and described in his letter (BOX 1) from April, 1918.
