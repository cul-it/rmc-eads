Content Description

The Palmer Canfield papers primarily document Canfield's final Prohibition post (from 1929) as "Dry Chief" for the "wet" leaning Albany District and material from his political and legal career, including his mayoral campaigns in Kingston (1913-1921), New York, dating from 1913 to 1931. The collection contains a typescript report and letters, newspaper clippings, correspondence, memorandums, invitations, mayoral candidate promotional material, business, and seasonal cards, a booklet, and a scrapbook album of newspaper clippings.
Biographical / Historical

Palmer Canfield was a New York politician and advocate of Prohibition, first as Special Assistant U.S. Attorney (1921) in the Southern District of New York and Legal Advisor at the Prohibition Department (1922) and later as Federal Prohibition Director for the State of New York (1923). Canfield had been an actively practicing attorney since 1904. He was also elected Mayor of Kingston, NY, from 1913 to 1921 on the "Honesty, Economy, Efficiency" platform. One of his main activities during his tenure as Mayor and before the National Prohibition, was the reduction of Kingston's salons to one-third of their original number (from 125 to 50). He was also one of the organizers of the Mayors Bureau of State, a Grand Chancellor of Knights of Pythia (1920-1921), and a member of the State and National Councils, Y.M.C.A., the executive committee N.Y. State Methodist Laymen's Association, the New York State Waterways Association, and more. He was married, and he had five children.

Anonymous letter against Dry Force
 1929 February 13
Scope and Contents
Provocative letter, titled " A Valentine for the Dry Force and their Commissioner Old Mother Hubbard, who is such a dry old maid his bones rattle," possibly by a group of people, signed as the Repealers of Prohibition. The sender(s) encourage Canfield to read Times Union's letters to the editor about Prohibition Law repeal, including the actual newspaper clippings.
