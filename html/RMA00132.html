This collection contains papers from Cornell University professor of history, Carl Becker, dating 1898-1956. It primarily contains lecture and course notes, correspondence, papers and photographs.

Carl Becker papers, #14-17-132. Division of Rare and Manuscript Collections, Cornell University Library.
Scope and content

This collection contains papers from Carl Becker dating 1898-1956. It contains lecture and course notes on the French Revolution and Napoleon, the beginnings of the American people, other aspects of modern European and American history, and the founding and history of Cornell University; correspondence with former students and other historians concerning the study and writing of history, especially Frederick Jackson Turner and Charles Homer Haskins; papers relating to Ezra Cornell and the early history of Cornell University; lectures; reviews; articles; notes on European and American history and for a bibliography of Becker's writings; six letters to Elias R. B. Willis, a Cornell librarian and friend, concerning the Manchester Guardian, the state and its role (1925), contrasts between Cornell University and the University of Chicago (1929), John Jay Chapman, his friend George Gemmill, and a poem satirizing academicians (1924); an undated letter to Laurence Bradford Packard; an autographed book; reprints; and biographical information including letters to and from Becker, photographs, and reminiscences about Becker by friends, relatives, and former students.

Series IV. Additional Papers
 1925-1945
Box 22 Folder 1
Correspondence between Alfred A. Knopf and Carl L. Becker.
 1925-04-08-1925-04-22
Scope and Contents
In the letters Knopf urges Becker to write a book on Benjamin Disraeli (Carl Van Doren made the suggestion to Knopf) but Becker politely turns down the offer.
Box 22 Folder 2
Correspondence between Alfred Knopf and Carl Becker
 1939-04-08-1942-02-05
Scope and Contents
Knopf is eager to publish works by Becker, but a series of articles are committed to the Yale University Press. An agreement is then made by Becker to publish The Declaration of Independence and much of the correspondence deals with changes or deletions in the manuscript before publication.
Box 22 Folder 3
General correspondence between Alfred Knopf and Carl Becker
 1943-03-23-1944-04-15
Scope and Contents
Mentioned is Becker's Penrose Lecture on Jefferson which appeared in the American Historical Review and the working over, publishing, and expected income (with suggestions from Knopf) of Becker's new book entitled, How New Will the Better World Be? The Circulating by Knopf of advance copies of the book to notable people like Samuel Eliot Morrison, Arthur M. Schlesinger, Jr., Archibald MacLeish, Carl Van Doren, and U.S. Supreme Court Justices Black, Frankfurter, Jackson, Douglas and Stone.
Box 22 Folder 4
Correspondence between Carl Becker and Alfred Knopf, and between Alfred Knopf, E. Blythe Stason (Dean of the University of Michigan Law School) and George H. Sabine of Cornell University.
 1944-05-12-1944-12-26
Scope and Contents
The letters deal with the publishing of Carl Becker's five William W. Cook Lectures on American Institutions: Lecture I, The American Political Tradition, Lecture II, Freedom of Speech and the Press, Lecture III, Freedom of Learning and Teaching, Lecture IV, Constitutional Government, Lecture V, Private Economic Enterprise
Box 22 Folder 5
Correspondence between Alfred Knopf and Carl Becker, and Alfred Knopf and E. Blythe Stason
 1945-01-01-1945-04-10
Scope and Contents
All concerning Becker's Michigan lectures (The Cook Lectures) and the publication and contract for the manuscript, "Freedom and Responsibility in the American Way of Life"
Box 22 Folder 5

 1945-04-10-1945-04-14
Box 22 Folder 5

 1945-04-17-1945-11-23

All in regard to the publication of Becker's manuscript, Freedom and Responsibility in the American Way of Life
